\title{Sentiment-Rating Incongruence in Sri Lanka Tourism Attraction Reviews}

\author{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Star ratings are widely used as ground-truth sentiment labels in opinion mining, yet their alignment with review text remains under-examined. This study investigates sentiment-rating incongruence, the mismatch between star ratings and textual sentiment, using 16,156 tourism attraction reviews from Sri Lanka. Textual sentiment is derived using a transformer-based pipeline, revealing incongruence in 18.6\% of reviews. This mismatch is structured across six directional patterns, with the Conservative Rater and Obligatory 5-Star patterns accounting for 66.7\% of all incongruent reviews. Incongruence varies significantly across venue types ($\chi^2 = 125.85$, $p < 0.001$), with museums showing the highest rates. A complementary modeling framework, Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and SHAP-based interpretation, identifies venue type, reviewer expertise, and review length as key predictors, while review delay shows no meaningful effect. Despite statistically significant predictors, predictive performance remains modest (AUC $\approx$ 0.58--0.61), indicating that incongruence is structured yet only partially explainable using observable features. These findings challenge the assumption that star ratings reliably represent textual sentiment and highlight implications for sentiment modeling and tourism platform design.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Tourism reviews have become a primary source of information guiding travel decisions, with most platforms presenting both a star rating and accompanying written text. In sentiment analysis research, these star ratings are commonly treated as ground-truth labels, providing a convenient mapping between numerical scores and sentiment polarity \cite{pang2008opinion,alaei2019tourism}. While this assumption simplifies model development, it remains relatively under-examined. In practice, ratings and written text do not always align, and when they diverge, the rating introduces systematic noise into the data that is subsequently learned and propagated by downstream models.

Such divergence is not necessarily the result of error. A visitor to the National Museum of Colombo may describe their experience positively, highlighting engaging exhibits and helpful staff, yet assign a moderate rating based on a stricter personal benchmark. Conversely, a visitor to a religious site in Kandy may assign a high rating out of cultural reverence while expressing only neutral or mildly critical sentiment in text. These cases reflect consistent and interpretable reviewer behaviour shaped by expectations, prior experience, and contextual factors. This study refers to this phenomenon as \textit{sentiment-rating incongruence} and treats it not as a labeling issue to be corrected, but as a structured behavioural pattern to be measured and explained.

Despite its implications, this phenomenon remains insufficiently examined. Prior work in tourism sentiment analysis continues to rely on star ratings as proxy labels, predominantly focusing on hotels and restaurants in well-studied destinations \cite{alaei2019tourism}. Studies in Sri Lankan contexts have identified incompatibility between ratings and textual sentiment in hotel reviews, demonstrating that such mismatches are both present and practically relevant \cite{abeysinghe2022selflearning,abeysinghe2021anuradhapura}. More broadly, research has examined alignment between ratings and textual sentiment in tourism and review platforms, often finding general agreement but with important contextual variations \cite{bigne2023aligned}. Work in related domains further suggests that rating-text inconsistencies can arise systematically and may be influenced by reviewer behaviour and contextual factors \cite{kwon2025beyond,chua2015helpfulness}.

There remains limited understanding of how frequently incongruence occurs, how it is structured across different forms, whether it varies across attraction types, and which reviewer-level and review-level characteristics contribute to its emergence. This study addresses these gaps using a dataset of 16,156 tourism attraction reviews from Sri Lanka spanning 2010 to 2023 \cite{sewwandi2023} across eleven venue categories. Sri Lanka provides a compelling context due to its relative absence in prior research and the influence of cultural norms, such as face-saving behaviour and reverence for sacred sites, on how experiences are expressed. By quantifying its prevalence, characterizing its structural patterns, examining variation across venue types, and identifying its key predictors, this work provides a systematic account of how sentiment is encoded differently across numerical and textual channels in tourism reviews.

\section{Related Work}
The foundational survey by Pang and Lee \cite{pang2008opinion} established sentiment analysis as a formal discipline and embedded a key assumption that numerical star ratings reliably represent the sentiment expressed in accompanying review text. While this assumption was acknowledged as imperfect, it has persisted as a practical convention within sentiment analysis and tourism research.

In the tourism domain, Alaei, Becken, and Stantic \cite{alaei2019tourism} reviewed sentiment analysis methods applied to travel and hospitality data and found that star ratings are widely used as weakly labelled ground truth without explicit validation. Their review further highlights that existing research is heavily concentrated on hotels and restaurants, with tourism attraction reviews in emerging destinations remaining substantially understudied.

Within the Sri Lankan context, Abeysinghe and Bandara \cite{abeysinghe2022selflearning,abeysinghe2021anuradhapura} documented incompatibility between TripAdvisor ratings and lexicon-derived sentiment in hotel reviews across multiple cities. Their findings demonstrate that rating-text mismatches are present and practically relevant, but their work focuses on hotel settings, relies on lexicon-based approaches, and primarily treats inconsistency as a labeling issue to be corrected. In contrast, the present study extends this investigation to tourism attraction reviews, employs a transformer-based sentiment pipeline, and frames incongruence as a behavioural phenomenon to be explained rather than corrected.

In broader tourism contexts, Bigne et al. \cite{bigne2023aligned} examined alignment between ratings and textual sentiment in attraction reviews and reported general consistency, while noting that such findings may not generalise across different destinations. Beyond tourism, Kwon et al. \cite{kwon2025beyond} demonstrate that rating-text inconsistency is not guaranteed even in general review datasets and varies with contextual factors.

Reviewer-level characteristics also play an important role in shaping how ratings and textual content are produced. Chua and Banerjee \cite{chua2015helpfulness} show that reviewer expertise influences the relationship between ratings and review content, suggesting that these signals carry independent informational value.

Taken together, the literature indicates that while rating-text mismatches are acknowledged, they remain insufficiently characterized in tourism attraction contexts, particularly in emerging destinations. To the best of our knowledge, no study has systematically characterized the full typology of directional incongruence patterns or identified their underlying structural predictors. This study addresses this gap using a large-scale dataset of tourism attraction reviews from Sri Lanka spanning multiple venue types.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
The analysis is based on the \textit{Tourism and Travel Reviews: Sri Lankan Destinations} dataset available on Mendeley Data \cite{sewwandi2023}, comprising 16,156 tourism attraction reviews collected across multiple regions in Sri Lanka. The dataset contains both structured attributes (e.g., ratings, location, reviewer metadata) and unstructured textual content, making it well-suited for analysing sentiment-rating relationships within real-world user-generated data. Sri Lanka represents a diverse emerging tourism destination, encompassing cultural, religious, natural, and heritage attractions, thereby creating a heterogeneous evaluation context in which rating-text inconsistencies are more likely to arise.

The preprocessing pipeline begins with data inspection, followed by missing value handling and duplicate removal to ensure data quality. Temporal features are extracted from \textit{Travel\_Date} and \textit{Published\_Date} through date parsing, including travel and publication year (\textit{Travel\_Year, Published\_Year}). A key derived feature, \textit{Review\_Delay\_Days}, is computed as the difference between publication and travel dates, with negative values corrected to maintain temporal consistency. It was included to test whether temporal distance between experience and review writing influences sentiment-rating incongruence.

Spatial information is standardized through rule-based location normalization. \textit{Province} and \textit{District} attributes are extracted from \textit{Location} using predefined mappings and pattern-based string parsing. \textit{User\_Country} information is inferred from \textit{User\_Location} through text normalization, dictionary-based matching, and the \textit{pycountry} library, with fallback rules applied to maximize coverage.

Additional text-based features, including \textit{Review\_Length}, are computed to capture review verbosity. A categorical \textit{Rating\_Class} variable is derived from the numerical rating to represent \textit{Positive (4--5)}, \textit{Neutral (3)}, and \textit{Negative (1--2)} sentiment categories. This binning strategy is commonly adopted in sentiment analysis studies, where ordinal rating scales are mapped to polarity classes for supervised analysis \cite{pang2008opinion,alaei2019tourism}, enabling direct comparison with text-derived sentiment. Finally, redundant and intermediate attributes, such as raw location strings, user identifiers, and original date fields, are removed after feature extraction. The resulting dataset is structured to support subsequent statistical analysis and predictive modeling.

\subsection{Sentiment Model Selection for Prediction}
To generate sentiment labels for the full corpus of 16,156 reviews, multiple candidate sentiment models were evaluated using a manually annotated subset of the dataset. A total of 1,000 reviews were randomly sampled and labeled into Positive, Neutral, and Negative classes based on the combined interpretation of both the review title and full text. The labeled dataset was stratified and split into 700 training samples and 300 test samples, ensuring class distribution consistency across splits. Two models (Cardiff RoBERTa and RoBERTa-base) were fine-tuned on the 700-review training set, while all candidate models, including pretrained and fine-tuned variants, were evaluated on the same held-out 300-review test set.

For all models, review titles and full text were concatenated to preserve contextual information. To handle long reviews exceeding model token limits, a chunking strategy was applied. During training, long reviews were split into overlapping chunks using a sliding window approach. During inference, predictions were generated for each chunk and aggregated via probability averaging to produce a final review-level sentiment prediction.

Performance was evaluated using standard classification metrics, with Macro F1 as the primary criterion due to class imbalance, and Accuracy and Weighted F1 as secondary measures. Among the evaluated models, \textit{cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment} (pretrained) was selected for corpus-wide sentiment prediction. While the fine-tuned \textit{roberta-base} model achieved the highest Macro F1-score (0.6984), the selected model demonstrated superior overall balance, achieving the highest Accuracy (0.8300) and Weighted F1-score (0.8051), while maintaining competitive Macro F1 (0.6918). Additionally, the pretrained model avoids reliance on task-specific fine-tuning and offers greater reproducibility across environments. All models were evaluated under a consistent pipeline, ensuring fair comparison and reliable sentiment labels for downstream analysis.

\subsection{Sentiment Prediction and Analytical Variables}
Using the selected sentiment model, a new categorical variable, \textit{Sentiment}, was generated for all reviews in the dataset. This variable represents the predicted polarity of each review based on the combined textual content of the title and review body. Afterwards, \textit{Title} and \textit{Text} columns were removed. To support subsequent statistical analysis, three core analytical variables were derived without additional predictive modeling:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Incongruent:} Incongruence (0/1) was defined by comparing \textit{Rating\_Class} and \textit{Sentiment}, with matching polarity treated as congruent and all other combinations as incongruent.
\item \textbf{Pattern:} A six-category variable captures directional mismatches, enabling finer analysis beyond binary incongruence.
\item \textbf{Reviewer\_Tier:} \textit{Reviewer\_Tier} was operationalized as an ordinal variable derived from \textit{User\_Contributions}. Given the highly right-skewed distribution of contribution counts (mean = 191.6, median = 54, max = 9010), a tiered categorization was adopted: \textit{Novice (0--5)}, \textit{Casual (6--20)}, \textit{Active (21--100)}, and \textit{Expert (101+)}.
\end{itemize}

This discretization produced a balanced representation across tiers (21--100: 37.2\%, 101+: 35.0\%, 6--20: 19.7\%, 0--5: 8.0\%), enabling stable statistical comparisons while preserving the ordinal structure of reviewer expertise. In addition, continuous variables, specifically \textit{Review\_Length} and \textit{Review\_Delay\_Days}, were log-transformed using $\log(1+x)$ to mitigate right-skewness and improve suitability for statistical modeling. All variables were computed deterministically from the processed dataset to ensure reproducibility. \textit{User\_Contributions} was dropped afterwards.

\subsection{Statistical Procedures and Model Validation}
The final phase integrates statistical testing, predictive modeling, and model interpretation to validate the analytical framework and identify key drivers of sentiment-rating incongruence. \textit{Location\_Name} and \textit{Located\_City} were excluded due to high cardinality and redundancy with higher-level spatial features (\textit{Province, Location\_Type}). \textit{User\_Country} and \textit{User\_Locale} were omitted due to sparsity and inconsistent formatting. \textit{Published\_Date}, \textit{Travel\_Date}, and \textit{Published\_Year} were not directly modeled because their information is captured through derived temporal variables (\textit{Travel\_Year}, \textit{Review\_Delay}), while \textit{Title\_Length} was excluded due to its high correlation with \textit{Review\_Length}.

\subsubsection{Exploratory and Association Testing}
Overall incongruence prevalence is computed as a proportion of total reviews. Associations between incongruence and categorical variables (\textit{Location\_Type}, \textit{Reviewer\_Tier}, \textit{Province}) are evaluated using the Chi-square test of independence. Effect sizes are reported using Cramer's V, and for \textit{Location\_Type} odds ratios are calculated relative to a reference category (National Parks). National Parks were chosen as the reference category due to their lowest observed incongruence rate and relatively uniform evaluation context.

For continuous variables (\textit{Review\_Length}, \textit{Review\_Delay}, \textit{Travel\_Year}), group differences between congruent and incongruent reviews are assessed using the Mann-Whitney U test due to non-normality. To control for multiple hypothesis testing, all p-values are adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg False Discovery Rate (FDR) procedure, with a significance threshold of $q<0.05$.

\subsubsection{Predictive Modeling Framework}
The target variable for all predictive models is binary Incongruence (0 = congruent, 1 = incongruent), as the primary objective is to identify and explain factors that lead to sentiment-rating mismatch. The variables \textit{Sentiment}, \textit{Rating}, and \textit{Rating\_Class} were excluded from the predictor set because they are directly used to construct the target variable.

The predictor set used three groups of dummy variables: \textit{Location\_Type}, \textit{Province}, and \textit{Reviewer\_Tier}. It also included temporal terms (\textit{Travel\_Year\_c}, \textit{Travel\_Year\_c2}) and review characteristics (\textit{log\_review\_length}, \textit{log\_review\_delay}). Categorical predictors were dummy encoded with National Parks, Southern Province, and Novice (0--5) as reference categories. Logistic Regression (Model 1A) was used as a baseline predictive model, while a statsmodels logit model (Model 1B) provided statistical inference via odds ratios and confidence intervals. A Random Forest (Model 2) captured nonlinear effects, and SHAP was applied for feature-level interpretation.

The logit model expresses results in terms of odds ratios (OR). OR greater than 1 indicates higher odds of incongruence compared to the baseline category, while OR less than 1 indicates lower odds.

\subsection{Model-Based Analysis and Interpretation}
\subsubsection{Model 1A: Logistic Regression (Baseline Discrimination)}
A class-balanced logistic regression model was trained using a leakage-free pipeline with five-fold stratified cross-validation. The model achieves a mean cross-validated AUC of $0.5890 \pm 0.0093$ and a held-out test AUC of 0.5840. This indicates modest but stable discriminative performance and serves as the baseline reference for linear predictive capacity.

\subsubsection{Model 1B: Explanatory Logit (Primary Inference Model)}
To identify statistically significant predictors of incongruence, a logistic regression model was fitted using \textit{statsmodels}. A total of 19 predictors are statistically significant ($p<0.05$), and the model yields a McFadden's pseudo-$R^2$ of 0.0203.

\textbf{Venue type effects:} Compared to National Parks, Museums (OR = 2.386), Beaches (OR = 2.171), Bodies of Water (OR = 2.022), and Religious Sites (OR = 1.492) show significantly higher odds of incongruence.

\textbf{Reviewer expertise:} Expert users (OR = 1.740), Active users (OR = 1.522), and Casual users (OR = 1.243) are each significantly more likely to produce incongruent reviews relative to Novices.

\textbf{Geographic effects:} Northern Province (OR = 1.787), Sabaragamuwa (OR = 1.641), North Central (OR = 1.601), and Central Province (OR = 1.586) show elevated odds relative to the reference province.

\textbf{Review characteristics:} Review length (OR = 1.219) has a significant positive association. Review delay (OR = 0.960) is weak and marginal.

\textbf{Temporal trend:} Travel year (OR = 0.946) is significant, while the quadratic term is non-significant.

\textbf{Non-significant predictors:} Gardens, Farms, Historic Sites, Eastern Province, and the quadratic time term.

\subsubsection{Model 2: Random Forest (Nonlinear Structure Assessment)}
To evaluate nonlinear relationships and interactions, a Random Forest model was trained on the same split. It achieves a mean CV AUC of 0.6023 and a test AUC of 0.6095, improving by +0.0256 over the logistic baseline. This indicates nonlinear and interaction effects are present but not dominant.

\subsubsection{SHAP Analysis: Interpreting Nonlinear Behaviour}
To interpret the nonlinear structure captured by the Random Forest, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis was applied.

Feature importance based on mean absolute SHAP values identifies review length (0.0306), expert reviewer tier (0.0273), travel year (0.0219), review delay (0.0203), and museums (0.0182) as the most influential predictors.

The SHAP summary plot (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_analysis}(b)) provides additional behavioural insights beyond feature ranking. In particular, review length exhibits a strong monotonic effect, where higher values (longer reviews) consistently produce positive SHAP contributions, indicating an increased likelihood of incongruence. Similarly, expert reviewers show a clear directional pattern, with high-expertise users contributing positively to incongruence predictions, while less experienced users contribute negatively.

Temporal effects are also evident: earlier travel years are associated with higher SHAP values, while more recent years shift predictions toward lower incongruence, confirming a decreasing trend over time consistent with the logit model.

Venue-specific effects display nonlinear threshold behaviour. For example, the presence of a museum (binary feature) leads to a sharp increase in SHAP values, indicating a strong context-specific impact rather than a gradual effect.

Additionally, the spread of SHAP values for key features such as review length and reviewer tier indicates interaction effects, where the impact of a feature varies depending on other contextual variables. These nonlinear and interaction patterns explain the modest performance improvement of the Random Forest over the logistic regression.

Importantly, SHAP complements rather than replaces the logit model: The logit establishes statistical significance and effect size, while SHAP reveals how these effects manifest in nonlinear, context-dependent behavioural patterns.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{The Scale of Incongruence Reflects Structured Context, Not Random Noise}
An overall incongruence rate of 18.6\%, approximately one in five reviews, indicates substantial mismatch between textual sentiment and star ratings. This rate is unlikely to be random misalignment alone and suggests underlying structural and contextual factors.

Several characteristics of Sri Lanka's tourism landscape provide plausible explanations. Religious and cultural heritage sites may induce social or normative pressure to assign high ratings even when textual experience is mixed, contributing to Obligatory 5-Star patterns. Museum and cultural venues require multidimensional evaluation that cannot be fully represented by a single scalar rating. A globally experienced reviewer base may also lead to divergence between detailed textual evaluation and conservative rating behaviour.

These findings establish that sentiment-rating incongruence is not random noise but a structured, context-dependent phenomenon embedded within the review ecosystem.

\subsection{Conservative Rater Behaviour as the Dominant Mechanism}
The Conservative Rater pattern, positive textual sentiment paired with moderate ratings, accounts for 38.4\% of all incongruent reviews and is the dominant mismatch mechanism.

This reflects a directional bias where reviewers express satisfaction in text but assign restrained stars. As a result, effectively positive experiences are labelled as neutral in structured datasets. Across reviewer tiers, Conservative Rater prevalence increases from 27.4\% among novices to 40.4\% among experts, consistent with a calibration-based behavioural mechanism.

\subsection{Venue Type as a Structural Moderator of Label Reliability}
Venue type emerges as a key structural determinant of incongruence. Relative to National Parks, museums show markedly higher odds (OR = 2.386), with elevated effects for beaches and bodies of water. This likely reflects differences in evaluative complexity, where multidimensional experiences are compressed into single star scores.

Conversely, religious sites may show elevated incongruence through social or cultural expectations that inflate ratings independently of textual detail. Label reliability is therefore not uniform across attraction types.

\subsection{Complementary Roles of Linear and Nonlinear Models}
The multi-model framework shows that linear and nonlinear approaches provide complementary evidence.

Model 1A provides stable linear discrimination (test AUC = 0.584). Model 1B identifies 19 statistically significant predictors with interpretable effect sizes. Model 2 improves test AUC to 0.6095, indicating nonlinear and interaction effects. The modest gain suggests nonlinear structure is present but secondary.

The overall AUC range ($\approx$ 0.58--0.61) should be interpreted as evidence that incongruence is systematic but only partially explainable using observable features, with remaining variation likely tied to latent behavioural factors.

SHAP analysis clarifies this by revealing nonlinear, context-dependent contributions for reviewer expertise, review length, and venue type. These findings complement, not contradict, the logit model.

\subsection{Review Delay as an Informative Null Result}
The absence of a significant review delay effect across bivariate tests, multivariate modeling, and SHAP ranking constrains interpretation. If memory decay were central, delayed reviews would show higher mismatch rates. No such pattern is observed.

This null result helps rule out temporal delay as a primary driver, narrowing the explanatory space toward reviewer characteristics and context.

\subsection{Behavioural Differentiation Across Reviewer Expertise}
A deeper typology view reveals systematic differences across reviewer expertise levels. Conservative Rater patterns increase from novice to expert users, while emotionally extreme patterns such as Harsh Deflator decline with expertise.

These findings reinforce a calibration-based interpretation and indicate that reviewer expertise fundamentally shapes how sentiment is encoded across textual and numerical channels.

\section{Limitations}
This study has several limitations.

First, the analysis is based on a single dataset of tourism reviews from Sri Lanka. While this context is valuable, findings may not generalize directly to other regions or mature tourism markets.

Second, the study relies primarily on structured features and does not incorporate deeper linguistic representations beyond sentiment classification.

Third, incongruence identification depends on text-derived sentiment labels, which are themselves subject to model error.

Fourth, modest predictive performance (AUC $\approx$ 0.58--0.61) indicates substantial unexplained variance, likely due to latent behavioural or contextual factors not observed in the dataset.

Finally, the study is observational and cannot establish causal relationships. Interpretations are based on statistical association and behavioural patterning.

\section{Conclusion}
This study demonstrates that sentiment-rating incongruence in tourism reviews is not random noise, but a structured and systematically occurring phenomenon shaped by reviewer behaviour and contextual evaluation complexity. By combining statistical inference, predictive modeling, and interpretable machine learning, the analysis shows that mismatch arises through identifiable mechanisms such as reviewer calibration, multidimensional evaluation constraints, and context-specific rating norms.

The findings establish that while incongruence is partially explainable through observable structural factors, it remains only weakly predictable, reflecting the inherently subjective and latent nature of human evaluative behaviour. This duality, structured yet not fully observable, highlights a fundamental limitation in treating star ratings as reliable ground truth for sentiment modeling.

For NLP systems, the presence of systematic and non-uniform label distortion calls for caution when using star ratings as supervision signals. For tourism platforms, aggregated ratings may underrepresent true visitor sentiment, particularly in contexts shaped by social norms or evaluative complexity.

More broadly, this study reframes rating-text inconsistency as a meaningful behavioural signal rather than an error to be eliminated, opening new directions for research at the intersection of human behaviour, cultural context, and machine learning. Future work should extend this framework across diverse geographic and cultural settings to better understand how social norms and evaluative practices influence the reliability of user-generated data.

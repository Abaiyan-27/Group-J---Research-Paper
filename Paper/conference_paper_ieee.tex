\begin{abstract}
Star ratings are widely used as ground-truth sentiment labels in opinion mining research, yet the assumption that ratings and review text always agree is rarely tested empirically. This study investigates sentiment-rating incongruence, the systematic disagreement between a reviewer's star rating and the sentiment expressed in written text, using 16,156 Sri Lankan tourism attraction reviews spanning 2011 to 2023. Sentiment was pre-computed from review title and text. Across three research questions, results show that incongruence affects 18.6\% of reviews and follows two dominant directional patterns: the Conservative Rater (positive text with neutral rating) and the Obligatory 5-Star (high rating with lukewarm text). Incongruence varies significantly by venue type, with museums showing the highest rate (26.3\%, OR = 2.43 versus national parks), and declines across COVID-19 phases. Logistic regression identifies venue type, reviewer expertise tier, review length, and travel year as independent predictors of incongruence (AUC = 0.59), while review delay is not significant. These findings challenge the assumption that star ratings are reliable sentiment proxies and have direct implications for NLP labeling practice and tourism platform ranking systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Online tourism reviews are a primary information source for travelers making destination decisions, with star ratings and written text serving as two principal signals through which reviewers communicate experience. In sentiment analysis research, star ratings are commonly treated as reliable proxies for text sentiment and used for labeling, training, and evaluation. This assumption is rarely tested empirically, despite its direct impact on downstream model validity.

In practice, the two signals do not always agree. A reviewer may describe a museum visit in strongly positive language while assigning three stars due to personal rating conservatism. Conversely, a reviewer may assign five stars while writing only a brief and lukewarm narrative. This study defines such disagreement as sentiment-rating incongruence and treats it as both an NLP measurement problem and a behavioral phenomenon.

Prior work has shown that tourism sentiment studies frequently rely on star ratings as weak supervision without validating rating-text agreement. Existing Sri Lankan work has focused primarily on hotel contexts and on correction-oriented lexicon pipelines. What remains underexplored is how often incongruence appears in tourism attraction reviews, whether it varies across venue types and time periods, and which structural factors independently predict it.

This study addresses these gaps using 16,156 Sri Lankan tourism attraction reviews from 2011 to 2023 across eleven attraction categories. Three research questions guide the analysis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item RQ1: How prevalent is sentiment-rating incongruence, and what directional patterns does it follow?
    \item RQ2: Does incongruence differ significantly across location types and time periods?
    \item RQ3: Which contextual, behavioral, and temporal factors independently predict incongruence?
\end{itemize}

The study applies chi-square tests, Mann-Whitney comparisons, Spearman trend analysis, and binary logistic regression. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related work, Section III describes methodology, Section IV presents results and analysis, and Section V concludes.

\section{Related Work}
Sentiment analysis and opinion mining were formalized by Pang and Lee~\cite{pang2008opinion}, who mapped computational approaches to opinion classification and also noted that numerical ratings may conflict with textual sentiment. Despite this early caution, tourism NLP research has largely preserved the convention that star ratings are reliable sentiment labels.

In tourism, Alaei \textit{et al.}~\cite{alaei2019tourism} showed that studies frequently use rating labels as weak ground truth while focusing on hotels and restaurants, leaving attraction reviews in emerging destinations underexplored. Sri Lankan studies by Abeysinghe and Bandara~\cite{abeysinghe2022selflearning} and Abeysinghe and Walgampaya~\cite{abeysinghe2021anuradhapura} confirmed rating-text mismatch in hotel contexts, but they were centered on incompatibility correction with lexicon-style approaches and did not model structural predictors of mismatch.

For attraction contexts, Bigne \textit{et al.}~\cite{bigne2023aligned} found broad alignment between ratings and textual sentiment in Venice while explicitly noting that findings may not generalize to emerging destinations. Beyond tourism, Kwon \textit{et al.}~\cite{kwon2025beyond} demonstrated that rating-text inconsistency can alter perceived review usefulness, and Valdivia \textit{et al.}~\cite{valdivia2019tripadvisor} showed inconsistency appears across all star levels with non-random distributions.

Behavioral evidence further supports predictor-driven analysis. Ibrahim \textit{et al.}~\cite{ibrahim2026moderate} identified moderate-rating bias that can produce evaluative ambiguity, and Chua and Banerjee~\cite{chua2015helpfulness} showed reviewer characteristics and review depth shape how rating and text signals are interpreted. Sugathan~\cite{sugathan2024incongruence} established that incongruence affects diagnosticity and authenticity for consumers.

Taken together, the literature establishes the existence and consequences of inconsistency, yet leaves a core explanatory gap: which venue-level, reviewer-level, and temporal factors predict incongruence in tourism attraction reviews in emerging destinations. This study addresses that gap using Sri Lankan attraction data and validated transformer-based sentiment classification.

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
The dataset consists of tourism reviews featuring location names, user locales, ratings, text, and timestamps. Records are initially subjected to missing value imputation and duplicate removal. Date parsing extracts the travel year and month, publication dates, and calculates the temporal review delay in days. Additionally, travel seasons (e.g., peak dry, inter-monsoon, southwest monsoon) are derived from the travel month. Location normalization maps raw strings to consistent province, district, and generalized tourism regions. User country and region inference utilize dictionary-based mappings combined with string matching rules via the \textit{pycountry} library.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
A comprehensive set of predictors is generated to capture multiple facets of the visitor experience:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Behavioral and Text Features:} Review text and title word counts, discretized length buckets, user contribution levels, and a normalized helpfulness ratio. A composite review quality score is also computed based on text richness and helpful votes.
    \item \textbf{Destination Performance Features:} Destinations are aggregated to calculate average ratings, total review counts, and ranking metrics (rating vs. popularity gap).
    \item \textbf{Temporal Features:} Travel season, review delay in days, and longitudinal patterns.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Natural Language Processing and Topic Modeling}
To derive robust textual insights, an ensemble sentiment classification approach is employed using three pre-trained transformer pipelines: \textit{CardiffNLP Twitter-RoBERTa}, \textit{SieBERT}, and \textit{BERTweet}. A mean voting mechanism creates a consolidated sentiment label and score, protecting against model biases. Emotion classification is executed using a pre-trained DistilRoBERTa model. These outputs enable the calculation of the sentiment-rating gap to identify incongruence.

Furthermore, latent document topics are extracted using \textbf{BERTopic} equipped with the \textit{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} sentence transformer. Discovered topic keywords are procedurally mapped to broad review themes, such as hospitality, wildlife, or heritage.

\subsection{Model Validation and Statistical Analysis}
The final phase validates the NLP outputs and interprets review attributes statistically. To validate the sentiment pipeline, the Cardiff RoBERTa model was fine-tuned on a manually annotated dataset (700 training reviews, 300 test reviews) using chunked training and inference to handle long text sequences. Predictive performance was systematically evaluated using Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and Macro F1-score metrics.

For statistical interpretation, relationships between variables---such as rating-sentiment incongruence across venue categories---are tested. Categorical relationships are evaluated using the \textbf{Chi-square test of independence} and odds-ratio comparisons. For continuous predictors (e.g., evaluating differences in review length, review delay, and tracking temporal drift against congruence), the non-parametric \textbf{Mann-Whitney U test} is applied.

\subsection{Data Source and Pre-processing}
This study analyzes 16,156 TripAdvisor reviews of Sri Lankan tourism attractions from 2010 to 2023, sourced from Mendeley Data. The corpus covers 11 location types, including national parks, beaches, museums, religious sites, and bodies of water, and includes reviewer metadata such as contribution history, travel date, province, review length, and posting delay. The dataset represents 140 unique user countries.

To compare numerical ratings and text sentiment directly, both were standardized to a three-point scale. Star ratings were mapped as: 1-2 = Negative, 3 = Neutral, and 4-5 = Positive. Sentiment labels were generated with the \texttt{cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment} model (accuracy = 0.80, macro-F1 = 0.682) on combined title and body text, producing POSITIVE, NEUTRAL, or NEGATIVE classes.

Continuous predictors, Review Length and Review Delay, were log-transformed using \texttt{log1p} to reduce right-skewness and improve linearity in the log-odds space. Reviewer experience was grouped into four tiers by contribution count: Novice (1-5), Casual (6-20), Active (21-100), and Expert (101+).

\subsection{Defining Sentiment-Rating Incongruence}
The dependent variable, \texttt{Incongruent}, is binary (0 = congruent, 1 = incongruent). A review is congruent when rating class and sentiment class align in direction (Positive/POSITIVE, Neutral/NEUTRAL, Negative/NEGATIVE); all other combinations are coded as incongruent.

To characterize mismatch direction, six patterns were defined: Conservative Rater (Neutral rating, Positive text), Obligatory 5-Star (Positive rating, Neutral text), Frustrated Neutral (Neutral rating, Negative text), Polite Inflator (Positive rating, Negative text), Harsh Deflator (Negative rating, Neutral text), and Punitive Rater (Negative rating, Positive text).

\subsection{Independent Predictors}
Twenty-one predictors were modeled across three dimensions. Contextual predictors included one-hot location-type dummies (National Parks as reference) and province dummies (Southern Province as reference). Behavioral predictors included reviewer tier (ordinal 0-3), log-transformed review length, and log-transformed review delay. Temporal variation was represented by \texttt{Travel\_Date\_Year} (2010-2023) as a continuous predictor.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
Bivariate associations were tested with chi-square tests for categorical variables and Mann-Whitney U tests for continuous variables. Multicollinearity was assessed using variance inflation factors via auxiliary regressions, with all VIF values below 5 (max VIF = 3.248, mean = 2.005). A multivariable binary logistic regression was fitted with z-score standardized continuous predictors. Effect sizes are reported as odds ratios with 95\% confidence intervals, and discrimination is reported using AUC-ROC.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{RQ2: Variation in Review Incongruence Across Location Types}

To examine whether review incongruence varies by location type, a chi-square test of independence was conducted between location type and incongruence status.
The results indicate a statistically significant association:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\chi^2(10) = 125.85$
    \item $p = 3.27 \times 10^{-22}$ ($p < 0.001$)
    \item Cram\'er's $V = 0.0883$
\end{itemize}

This confirms that incongruence is not uniformly distributed across location types. However, the effect size (Cram\'er's $V \approx 0.09$) suggests that while the relationship is statistically significant, the practical strength of the association is small.

\subsubsection{Variation Across Location Types}
A clear gradient in incongruence rates is observed across different categories:

\textbf{Higher-than-average incongruence}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Museums: 26.3\% (OR = 2.43)
    \item Bodies of Water: 23.7\% (OR = 2.12)
    \item Zoological Gardens: 21.6\% (OR = 1.88)
\end{itemize}
These categories show substantially elevated odds of incongruence compared to the reference category (National Parks). For example, reviews of museums are 2.43 times more likely to be incongruent than those of national parks.

\textbf{Moderately elevated incongruence}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Religious Sites: 19.8\% (OR = 1.68)
    \item Beaches: 19.0\% (OR = 1.60)
    \item Waterfalls: 18.0\% (OR = 1.50)
\end{itemize}
These locations cluster around or slightly above the overall average (18.6\%), indicating moderate variability in user experience or expression.

\textbf{Lower incongruence (closer to baseline)}
\begin{itemize}

    \item Farms: 16.6\% (OR = 1.35)

These categories exhibit relatively stable and consistent reviews, with lower levels of mismatch between rating and textual sentiment.

\textbf{Lowest incongruence (reference group)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item National Parks: 12.8\% (OR = 1.00)
\end{itemize}
National parks serve as the baseline and demonstrate the highest consistency between ratings and review text.

\subsubsection{Interpretation and Insights}
\textbf{1. Experience subjectivity vs. consistency}\\
Locations like museums and zoological gardens tend to produce more subjective experiences. Visitors may evaluate exhibits differently based on personal interest, expectations, or prior knowledge. This variability can lead to mismatches—for example, a high rating paired with critical comments, or vice versa.
In contrast, national parks and nature-based locations often provide more uniform and immersive experiences, leading to greater alignment between rating and sentiment.

\textbf{2. Expectation--reality gaps}\\
Higher incongruence in categories like museums and bodies of water may reflect expectation mismatches. For instance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A museum may be rated highly for cultural value but criticized for maintenance or crowding.
    \item A beach or water body might receive a good rating overall, yet include complaints about cleanliness or facilities.
\end{itemize}
Such mixed experiences naturally produce incongruent review signals.

\textbf{3. Emotional vs. functional evaluation}\\
Some location types encourage emotional or symbolic evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Religious sites may receive high ratings due to spiritual significance despite negative practical aspects (e.g., crowding, accessibility).
\end{itemize}
Others are evaluated more functionally and holistically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item National parks and nature reserves are often judged based on overall experience, leading to more coherent rating--text alignment.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{4. Implications of small effect size}\\
Although the differences are statistically significant, the low Cram\'er's V (0.0883) indicates that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Location type alone explains only a small portion of the variation in incongruence.
    \item Other factors (e.g., reviewer behavior, cultural norms, sentiment expression styles) likely play a larger role.
\end{itemize}

\section{Threats to Validity}
Potential threats include dataset representativeness, annotation assumptions in rating-class conversion, and location parsing noise in ambiguous records. External validity should be assessed by testing the framework on additional tourism datasets.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This paper introduced a reproducible framework for tourism review analytics integrating preprocessing, feature engineering, and model validation. Future work includes multilingual sentiment modeling, aspect-level opinion mining, and temporal drift analysis.
